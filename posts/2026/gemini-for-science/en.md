We have a comfortable story about where AI fits into knowledge work: the machine does the grunt work, the human keeps the creative spark. The source I want to examine quietly inverts that story. Google's **Gemini for Science** — the I/O 2026 announcement of Hypothesis Generation (built on Co-Scientist), Computational Discovery (built on AlphaEvolve), and Literature Insights (built on NotebookLM) — is an attempt to automate not the drudgery of science but its *ideation*. It points the machine at the supposedly human core: forming the hypothesis. That inversion is the whole story, and it is worth taking seriously rather than celebrating or fearing on reflex.

## Deep dive

Map the three tools onto the actual scientific method and the design intent snaps into focus. Literature Insights is the literature review — searching the corpus, structuring it into comparable attributes, finding the gaps. Hypothesis Generation is conjecture — Co-Scientist runs a multi-agent "idea tournament" that generates, debates, and evaluates hypotheses. Computational Discovery is experimentation — AlphaEvolve generates and scores thousands of code variations in parallel to test modeling approaches in silico. Three of the method's stages, instrumented. What is conspicuously *not* in the box is the wet-lab confrontation with reality and the choice of which question matters. The announcement says so almost in passing: AI handles the complex tasks so researchers can "focus on identifying and tackling the most impactful scientific problems." Read that carefully — the human is left with problem-selection and judgment; the machine takes ideation, search, and synthesis.

This is the inversion, and it is the opposite of the consoling narrative. The parts being automated here — generating hypotheses, synthesizing thousands of papers, inventing novel modeling approaches — are the *divergent, generative* acts we have always called creative. What is left to the human is the *convergent, evaluative* work: deciding which problem is worth solving, and whether the machine's output is true. The machine is encroaching on imagination; the human is being promoted to editor. Whether that is a demotion or a liberation is the real debate, but the announcement should not be allowed to pretend it is merely handing over chores.

The two engines deserve technical precision because they work differently. **Co-Scientist** is a generate-and-test search over *hypotheses*, with multi-agent debate as the selection pressure and "deep verification" plus clickable citations as the brake on hallucination — and, notably, it is validated in a Nature paper published the same day. **AlphaEvolve** is an evolutionary search over *programs*: an LLM proposes code mutations, an evaluator scores them, selection iterates, thousands of variants in parallel. The thing to understand about AlphaEvolve is that it is only ever as good as its fitness function. It does not know truth; it optimizes a metric. Point it at a problem with a clean computational scorer — supply-chain cost at BASF, forecast error in solar prediction, training speed at Klarna — and it is genuinely powerful. Point it at a problem where the metric is a *proxy* for the real scientific goal, and fast parallel search will optimize the proxy straight past the truth. That is Goodhart's law with a compute budget.

Which exposes the speed-versus-accuracy relationship the brief asks about, and the answer is sharper than "faster but riskier." These tools do not make science faster uniformly; they relocate the bottleneck. They make *hypotheses* cheap — Co-Scientist can generate more in an afternoon than a lab in a year — but they do nothing to make *validation* cheap. Plausibility is not truth, and a beautifully cited, debate-tournament-winning hypothesis is still a conjecture until reality rules on it. The AK2 example in the announcement is the honest tell: Science Skills compressed a structural analysis from hours to minutes and produced "potential mechanisms" for a rare genetic disease. *Potential* is doing the load-bearing work in that sentence. The speed is real and the output is a lead, not a finding. So the net effect of cheap ideation is to shift the scarce resource downstream — from having ideas to verifying them — and that is where the medical payoff actually lives: AlphaFold's 3 million researchers, Stanford on liver fibrosis, Imperial on antimicrobial resistance, the AK2 mechanism. Each is a hypothesis the machine surfaced and humans (and reality) must still confirm.

## Debate

**Does automating ideation erode critical thinking and reshape creativity?** I think the honest answer splits by career stage. For the established expert, this is a force multiplier in the literal sense — it amplifies judgment the scientist already has. For the *forming* scientist, it is more dangerous, because intuition is built precisely through the slow, manual slog the tools remove: reading the papers, hitting the dead ends, wrestling the data. A researcher who can prompt Co-Scientist but has never personally struggled to a hypothesis may end up able to operate the instrument without being able to second-guess it. Creativity does not vanish; it migrates — from generating ideas to curating and combining machine-generated ones. That is a real skill, but it is a different one, and pretending the transition is costless is the mistake. The printing press and the calculator provoked the same fear and science thrived — but each also retired a faculty we used to consider essential, and we should be clear-eyed that hypothesis intuition may be next.

**Speed versus accuracy, and who is responsible for the result.** These two questions are the same question once you see that the machine optimizes metrics and humans own truth. An algorithm cannot be accountable — it has no standing to be wrong in the way science requires someone to be answerable. So responsibility must remain human, which means the human who selects the problem and validates the output is the author of the discovery, not the system that proposed it. The announcement's careful language — "partner," "collaborator," "force multiplier" — is doing exactly this work, keeping the human as principal. I think that is correct, but it implies a duty the piece does not state: if you take the credit, you own the error, including the errors hidden inside a model's confident, well-cited hypothesis. The ethical failure mode of *total* reliance on autonomous research agents is not a rogue AI; it is a thousand labs efficiently pursuing the same plausible-but-wrong machine hypothesis, error propagating at the speed of the tool, with no human having personally reasoned their way to the claim and therefore no one positioned to doubt it.

**How original is any of this, really?** Less than the framing suggests, and that is not a dismissal. Automated hypothesis generation from literature dates to Swanson's work in the 1980s; closed-loop robot scientists that form and test hypotheses go back to Ross King's "Adam" and "Eve" around 2009; evolutionary program search is decades old. The genuinely new claim is the announcement's own thesis — that discovery will come from *general agents*, not "narrow, specialized models." That is a real, contestable bet, and it is the most interesting sentence in the piece precisely because Google is betting *against the paradigm it pioneered*.

## Critique

The strongest thing in the announcement is also the source of its deepest tension, and a more rigorous version would confront it head-on. The thesis is that the future is general agents, not narrow specialists — yet the evidence marshaled for that future is AlphaFold, the most celebrated *narrow specialist* in the history of scientific ML. The piece celebrates the old paradigm's trophy while announcing its retirement, and never reconciles the two. Are general agents actually outperforming specialized models on hard science, or are they a more convenient interface to capability that still, underneath, depends on specialists like AlphaFold and AlphaGenome? The announcement assumes the answer; it does not show it.

The second gap is a quiet recursion that deserves scrutiny. Google is building tools to *generate* science (Co-Scientist, AlphaEvolve) and, in the same breath, tools to *peer-review* it — PAT and ScholarPeer, piloted at ICML, STOC, and NeurIPS. Automating both the production and the validation of science thins the human checkpoint at exactly the moment cheap ideation makes that checkpoint most necessary. If an AI proposes the hypothesis and an AI reviews the paper, where precisely does independent human judgment enter? The trusted-tester community "from PhD students to Nobel laureates" is the announced answer, and it is a good instinct, but it is described as a stamp of credibility rather than a defined process with teeth.

Third, the evidence is the now-familiar mix of one genuinely strong signal and a lot of testimonial. The Nature publications for ERA and Co-Scientist are the real thing — external, peer-reviewed validation, and the piece should have led with them and said far more about what they actually demonstrate. Instead they sit late in the text beneath a parade of partner logos (BASF, Klarna, Bayer, Daiichi Sankyo) that establish adoption, not correctness. For an announcement whose entire subject is scientific rigor, leaning on logos over results is a self-inflicted wound.

## Summary

Gemini for Science instruments three stages of the scientific method — literature review (Literature Insights), hypothesis formation (Co-Scientist's multi-agent idea tournament), and computational experimentation (AlphaEvolve's evolutionary program search) — and in doing so inverts the comfortable story about AI and knowledge work: it automates *ideation*, the supposedly human core, and leaves humans with problem-selection, validation, and accountability. The crucial technical truth is that these engines optimize metrics, not truth — AlphaEvolve is only as good as its fitness function — so they make hypotheses cheap without making validation cheap, relocating the scientific bottleneck downstream to verification, exactly where the medical payoffs (the AK2 mechanism, liver fibrosis, antimicrobial resistance, AlphaFold's vaccine work) still require human and empirical confirmation. The honest worries are deskilling for forming scientists, error propagation under total reliance on autonomous agents, and an accountability principle the piece implies but never states: take the credit, own the error. The framing's boldest and least-defended claim is that discovery will come from general agents rather than narrow specialists — a bet Google makes while citing AlphaFold, the narrow specialist, as its proof. Most original, most contestable, and most worth watching: whether the lab that perfected the narrow superhuman model is right that the future belongs to the generalist agent.
