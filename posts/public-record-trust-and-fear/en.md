Most AI discourse is written by people who build the systems for people who study them. The source I'm examining is the rare exception: the first wave of the **Anthropic Public Record**, a nationally representative survey of 51,993 Americans fielded in late 2025, deliberately reaching the people every other dataset misses — those who do not use AI at all. What it found is not a verdict but a set of productive contradictions: a public that wants AI to cure cancer and fears it will take their jobs, that distrusts the companies building it more than any other institution, and whose fear of the technology falls the more they actually touch it.

## Deep dive

Start with the single most uncomfortable number in the report, because everything else organizes around it: only **15% of Americans trust AI companies** to make decisions about how AI is developed and used. That is the lowest score for any institution the survey tested — below the federal government (20%), below state and local government (19%), below international bodies (20%), and not even close to independent experts, who sit at 43%. The gap I want to name precisely, because the source's Italian-style framing of "experts vs. companies" is too coarse: it is not that the public distrusts the *field*. They trust the scientists nearly three times as much as the firms. What they distrust is the alignment of corporate incentives with the public good — a distinction the industry would be wise not to flatter itself out of.

Now hold that against the hopes, because the contradiction is the finding. The number-one thing Americans want from AI is **curing diseases — 48%**, a full twelve points clear of the next item. They are not dreaming of chatbots or companionship; therapy and relief from loneliness ranked dead last. They want AI pointed at cancer and Alzheimer's. So we have a population that distrusts the builders and simultaneously holds an almost moral expectation that those same builders deliver a medical miracle. That is not incoherence — it is the ordinary structure of how people relate to powerful institutions they cannot opt out of. You can distrust pharmaceutical companies and still want the drug.

The fears sharpen the picture, and here the source's most original contribution lives. **Job loss is the top fear at 64%** — the number-one concern in every single state, from Iowa (71%) to Mississippi (57%), bipartisan, evenly spread across households with and without children. But two cross-cuts turn a banal headline into something genuinely interesting. First, fear of job loss *rises* with education: postgraduates are nearly ten points more worried than those with a high-school education or less. The people most alarmed are precisely the knowledge workers whose tasks overlap most with what frontier models are now asked to do. Second, and pulling the opposite way, fear *falls* with use: Americans who use AI daily at work worry at 54%, those who never use it at 70%. The same sixteen-point gradient appears for cognitive dependency (46% daily-users vs. 62% never-users).

This usage gradient is the spine of the whole report, and it forces an interpretive fork the source is admirably honest about. Either hands-on contact *teaches* — you learn AI's limits, you learn to augment rather than be replaced, and the fear deflates as a function of knowledge — or the people who already use AI daily are simply a different kind of person (younger, urban, employed, college-educated, self-described early adopters) whose optimism predates their usage. The integrated users — the ~6% who use AI daily for both work and life — are explicitly framed as "a preview of where mainstream opinion is headed." That is the load-bearing claim of the document, and it is a selection-effect landmine: a preview only if usage causes the optimism, a mirage if optimism causes the usage. The data cannot yet distinguish them, and the report knows it.

One more pairing deserves attention because it is the quiet rebuttal to techno-optimism. Cognitive dependency, the second-ranked fear at 56%, turns out to be **almost entirely anticipatory**: of those worried about it, only about a fifth would actually feel significant disruption if AI vanished tomorrow — while a third of those *not* worried would. People fear a dependency they don't yet have, and discount one they already do. And the occupations most worried? Educators and arts-and-design workers — the same educators who, in Anthropic's parallel qualitative study, were 2.5 to 3 times more likely to report witnessing cognitive atrophy firsthand, presumably in their students. The fear isn't naive. It's a forward-deployed observation from the people positioned to see it first.

## Debate

Let me argue the tensions rather than smooth them, because the report's value is in its frictions.

**For taking the public's regulatory demand at face value:** the appetite is real and bipartisan. 71% want government involved in AI — 79% of Democrats, 68% of Republicans, a supermajority in every state from DC (81%) to Hawaii (63%). And the demands are coherent with the fears: the public wants companies held legally liable for harm (47%) and safety prioritized over growth (44%) far more than they want development slowed outright (27%). That is a sophisticated position, not a reflexive one — it says *build it, but bear the cost when it breaks*, which maps cleanly onto liability law for every other dangerous product.

**Against treating that demand as a clear mandate:** look at where support actually clears a majority for more-than-minimal government action — only two domains, privacy (56%) and child safety (52%). On most of the eight governance areas tested, enthusiasm for an active state thins out fast. So the 71% is a mile wide and, in places, an inch deep: broad agreement that *someone* should act, much shallower agreement on *what* they should do. A regulator reading this survey as a blueprint would find the public agrees on the verb and not the object.

**On the paradox the guidance rightly flags — distrust of companies coexisting with daily reliance on their products:** I don't think it's a paradox at all, and pretending it is misreads people. Trust-in-stewardship and trust-in-utility are different instruments. I can find a tool indispensable and still believe the company behind it would sell my data or cut a safety corner under growth pressure. The 15% figure is not a usage referendum; it's a governance verdict. The error would be for the industry to point at adoption curves as if they refuted the trust number. Adoption is not consent to be governed by the adopted.

**On who speaks for the non-users:** this is the survey's real democratic contribution and its sharpest unresolved tension. Every prior Anthropic dataset — the 81,000-user study, the Economic Index — listens to people already inside the tent. This one deliberately reaches outside it, and the outsiders are the most fearful and the least trusting. If the integrated-user "preview" thesis is right, their fear will mellow with exposure and policy can afford to wait. If it's wrong — if the early adopters were never representative — then building policy around the optimists is building it around a self-selected vanguard while the majority's concerns go structurally underweighted. The ethical stance is to treat the non-user's fear as data, not as a deficit to be corrected by adoption.

## Critique

As a piece of public-facing research communication, this is strong work with three soft spots worth naming.

First, the **logical architecture genuinely guides the reader between hope and fear** — opening with hopes, moving to fears, then resolving into governance — and that arc is the right one. But the report front-loads its summary bullets and only later supplies the cross-cuts (education, usage) that make the numbers mean something. The 64% job-loss figure is nearly inert on its own; it's the *gradient by usage and education* that's the insight. I'd lead with the gradient and treat the headline number as scaffolding, not the reverse.

Second, the **trust-deficit message is clear but under-interrogated.** "Only 15% trust AI companies" is delivered as a clean fact, but the survey is published *by* an AI company, which both heightens its credibility (they're reporting their own low marks) and demands more reflexivity than the text offers. The closing line — "the direction AI takes should not be set only by the companies building it" — is admirable, but a careful reader wants the obvious question addressed head-on: what does it mean for the most-distrusted institution to be the one defining the survey instrument, the harm taxonomy, and the policy frameworks the results are then used to endorse? The report gestures at this; it doesn't confront it.

Third, the **coherence between measured fears and proposed remedies is asserted more than demonstrated.** Anthropic's own Advanced AI Framework (independent safety testing, transparency, recall authority) and Economic Policy Framework are presented as responsive to the findings — and they plausibly are — but the survey didn't test *those* proposals against alternatives. The public said "liability" and "safety over growth"; the policy answer is a specific regulatory architecture the public was never shown. That's a reasonable inference, but it should be labeled as the company's interpretation, not as what the data demands. The integrity move would be to publish the gap between what was asked and what is proposed.

What the report does exceptionally well, and what every other AI-attitudes dataset should copy, is reaching the non-user and resisting the urge to explain their fear away. The honesty about the integrated-user selection effect — naming it as "probably reflecting the outlook of early adopters" rather than proof that exposure cures fear — is the single most credible sentence in the document.

## Summary

The first Anthropic Public Record surveys ~52,000 Americans and finds a public in productive contradiction: it wants AI to cure disease (48%, the top hope by a wide margin) while fearing it will destroy jobs (64%, the top fear in every state), and it trusts the companies building AI less than any other institution (15%, versus 43% for independent experts). The signal finding is a usage gradient — fear of both job loss and cognitive dependency falls steadily the more a person actually uses AI — but whether that reflects learning or mere self-selection is the unresolved crux, and the report is honest that its "heavy users preview the mainstream" thesis rests on it. The public's regulatory appetite is broad (71%, bipartisan) but shallow outside privacy and child safety, and its core asks — liability for harm, safety over growth — are coherent with its fears even where the proposed remedies run ahead of what was measured. The distrust-of-companies-but-reliance-on-products tension is not a paradox: utility is not consent to be governed. The report's real achievement is methodological courage — it listened to the people who don't use AI, and let their fear stand as evidence rather than error.
